Tips

Measuring ingredients:

When measuring dry ingredients, such as flour or powdered sugar, spoon the ingredient into the measuring cup. This helps prevent packing the ingredients, which will offput the ratio in the recipe.

Sifting:

Sifting dry ingredients is ideal to get a good mixture when making cakes and other batters. It helps eliminate any lumps and will also make sure that each ingredient is evenly distributed throughout the batter.

You will absolutely need to sift powdered sugar when making frostings. It is best to sift the sugar right into the buttercream as you are making this. This will prevent lumpy frosting from forming.

Beating Eggs / Egg whites:

It is best to have room temperature eggs when a recipe calls for beaten eggs or whisked egg whites, especially for recipes such as angel food cake and meringue. Allowing the eggs to come to room temperature allows the eggs to whip up in a greater volume.

It is also important not to over-beat the batter once the eggs have been added. Over-beating will cause the cake to come out rubbery.

Butter:

Unless the recipe tells you to melt the butter, DO NOT melt it. Instead, leave the butter out at room temperature for at least one hour prior to making the recipe. This is crucial when making butter cream, as it will whip up much better and will give you a nice smooth and light frosting.

Fold vs Whisk vs Stir vs Beat:

Language is important when it comes to recipes, and there is a difference between “beating” something in and “stirring” it in.

Folding is done by using a spatula and gently ‘folding’ the two mixtures together. This is usually called for when mixing meringue into a batter. This is so air beaten into the meringue is not lost with vigorous stirring.

Whisking is done using a hand whisk or a whisk attachment. This is done to try to incorporate air into whatever is being whisked. This is crucial for meringue and some cake recipes.

Stirring is done by simply mixing in the ingredient either slowly by hand or on a slow/low setting on a stand mixer. I would not recommend using an electric hand mixer for this as the lowest settings are usually still too fast.

Beating is usually done by using an electric hand mixer or a stand mixer on a medium to high setting (the recipe should tell you what speed, and if it doesn’t, stick with medium to medium-low). This is usually done to incorporate ingredients well together, such as when combining dry and wet ingredients, with cakes, or when softening butter for buttercream frosting.

Preparing Pans:

I will always recommend using parchment paper when baking. It will save so much time and keep your cakes and other baked goods looking great.

To use parchment paper, grease the pans with butter, margarine, or shortening (I recommend shortening as its cheaper and lasts longer than butter). Cut out a piece of parchment paper that will fit on the bottom of the pan and then place it in the greased pan. The grease should help the parchment paper stick. Then use flour to coat the edges of the pan. If the pan is deep, I would recommend using parchment paper to line the sides instead of flour.

Decorating:

Always let baked goods cool before adding any kind of decoration. If you try to decorate something that is still even slightly warm, the decoration will likely melt. Always make sure it is completely cool before decorating.

Cooling Cakes:

Cool cakes and other baked goods on a wire rack on the counter. Do not place hot cakes or other baked goods in the fridge or freezer to cool faster. This will cause condensation to build on your fresh baked goodness and will cause to get soggy.
